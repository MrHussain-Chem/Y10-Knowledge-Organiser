Rates of Reaction

Measuring Rates of Reaction

Mean rate = Amount of reactant used ÷ Time
Mean rate = Amount of product formed ÷ Time
Units: g/s, cm³/s, mol/s
Worked Example: If 24 cm³ of CO₂ is produced in 40s:
Rate = 24 ÷ 40 = 0.6 cm³/s

Methods to Measure Rate

  • Gas collection: Volume of gas produced over time (gas syringe/measuring cylinder)
  • Mass loss: Decrease in mass as gas escapes (digital balance)
  • Precipitation: Time for solution to become opaque (cross disappears)
  • Colour change: Time for colour to appear/disappear (colorimeter)

Factors Affecting Rate

Temperature ↑
• Particles move faster
• More frequent collisions
• More particles exceed Ea
• Rate approximately doubles per 10°C rise
Concentration ↑
• More particles per unit volume
• More frequent collisions
• Directly proportional to rate
Surface Area ↑
• More particles exposed
• More collision sites available
• Powder > lumps > large pieces
Catalyst
• Provides alternative pathway
• Lower activation energy
• Same final position, faster rate
• Not used up in reaction

Collision Theory

  • Particles must collide to react
  • Collisions must have sufficient energy (≥ activation energy)
  • Particles must have correct orientation
  • Successful collisions = collisions that lead to reaction
Key Understanding: Explain rate changes using collision theory. Higher temperature increases both frequency AND energy of collisions.

Interpreting Rate Graphs

  • Steeper gradient = faster rate
  • Horizontal line = reaction finished (limiting reactant used up)
  • Tangent to curve = instantaneous rate at that time
  • Same final volume/mass = same amount of product (same limiting reactant)
Exam Tip: Always draw tangent lines with a ruler and calculate gradient: Δy/Δx

Key Terms - Rates & Energy

Key Terms Definitions
Rate of Reaction How fast reactants are used up or products formed (per unit time)
Activation Energy (Ea) Minimum energy needed for particles to react successfully
Catalyst Substance that speeds up reaction without being used up
Exothermic Reaction that transfers energy to surroundings (ΔH negative)
Endothermic Reaction that takes in energy from surroundings (ΔH positive)
Bond Energy Energy needed to break one mole of bonds (always positive)
Equilibrium Forward and reverse reaction rates are equal (dynamic process)
Le Chatelier's Principle System responds to oppose changes in conditions
Common Mistake: Bond breaking requires energy (endothermic), bond forming releases energy (exothermic)

Energy Changes

Exothermic vs Endothermic

  • Exothermic: Energy transferred to surroundings (temperature rises, ΔH negative)
  • Endothermic: Energy taken from surroundings (temperature falls, ΔH positive)
Energy Profile Diagrams
Exothermic: Products lower than reactants (energy released)
Endothermic: Products higher than reactants (energy absorbed)
Peak = Activation Energy (same for both directions without catalyst)

Bond Energy Calculations

Energy change = Σ(bonds broken) - Σ(bonds formed)
ΔH = Energy required - Energy released
Worked Example: H₂ + Cl₂ → 2HCl
Bonds broken: H-H (436 kJ/mol) + Cl-Cl (243 kJ/mol) = 679 kJ/mol
Bonds formed: 2 × H-Cl (431 kJ/mol) = 862 kJ/mol
ΔH = 679 - 862 = -183 kJ/mol (Exothermic)
  • Negative ΔH = Exothermic reaction (energy released)
  • Positive ΔH = Endothermic reaction (energy absorbed)

Examples of Energy Changes

  • Exothermic: Combustion, neutralisation, respiration, oxidation
  • Endothermic: Photosynthesis, thermal decomposition, dissolving ammonium salts
  • Everyday uses: Hand warmers (iron oxidation), sports injury packs (ammonium nitrate)

Reaction Profiles & Catalysts

  • Activation energy: Energy barrier that must be overcome
  • Catalysts: Lower activation energy
  • Higher temperature: More particles exceed activation energy
  • Different pathways: Catalysts provide alternative route with lower Ea
Key Understanding: Catalysts speed up rate of reaching equilibrium but don't change the equilibrium position.

Chemical Equilibria

Reversible Reactions

  • Products can react to reform reactants
  • Shown with ⇌ symbol
  • Example: NH₄Cl(s) ⇌ NH₃(g) + HCl(g)
  • Closed system: No matter can enter or leave

Dynamic Equilibrium

  • Rate of forward reaction = Rate of reverse reaction
  • Concentrations of reactants and products remain constant
  • Only occurs in closed system
  • Dynamic: Reactions continue but no net change

Le Chatelier's Principle

If conditions change, equilibrium shifts to oppose the change

Effects of Changing Conditions

  • Concentration ↑: Equilibrium shifts away from increased substance
  • Concentration ↓: Equilibrium shifts towards decreased substance
  • Temperature ↑: Equilibrium shifts in endothermic direction
  • Temperature ↓: Equilibrium shifts in exothermic direction
  • Pressure ↑: Equilibrium shifts to side with fewer gas molecules
  • Catalyst: No effect on position, speeds up both reactions equally
Worked Example: N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g) ΔH = -92 kJ/mol
• ↑ Pressure: Shifts right (4 moles → 2 moles)
• ↑ Temperature: Shifts left (endothermic direction)
• Remove NH₃: Shifts right to replace NH₃

Industrial Applications - Haber Process

N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃ (ΔH = -92 kJ/mol)
  • High pressure (200 atm): Favours NH₃ formation (fewer molecules)
  • Moderate temperature (450°C): Compromise between yield and rate
  • Iron catalyst: Increases rate to reach equilibrium faster
  • NH₃ removed: Shifts equilibrium right, converted to fertilizer
Exam Tip: Always count gas molecules on each side to predict pressure effects.

Structure and Bonding

Bond Type Formation Properties Examples
Ionic Metal transfers electrons to non-metal. Electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions. High m.p./b.p., conduct when molten/dissolved, brittle, soluble in water NaCl, MgO, CaF₂
Covalent Non-metals share pairs of electrons. Strong bonds within molecules, weak forces between. Low m.p./b.p., don't conduct electricity (except for graphite, graphene and fullerenes), often gases/liquids at room temperature H₂O, CO₂, CH₄
Metallic Positive metal ions in 'sea' of delocalised electrons. Good conductors, malleable, ductile, high m.p., lustrous Fe, Cu, Al

Giant Structures

  • Giant Ionic lattice: Strong electrostatic force of attraction between oppositely charged ions
  • Giant Metallic lattice: Strong electrostatic force of attraction between positively charged metal ion and sea of delocalised electrons
  • Giant Covalent lattice: Many strong covalent bonds

Examples of Giant Covalent Lattices

  • Diamond: Each C is covalently bonded to 4 others, very hard and doesn't conduct
  • Graphite: Layers of carbons arranged in hexagons, soft as layers can slide and can conduct electricity
  • Graphene: Single layer of graphite
  • Silicon dioxide (silica): Each Si is covalently bonded to 4 oxygen atoms, very hard

Carbon Allotropes

  • Allotropes: Different arrangements of the same element
  • Examples: Graphite, graphene, diamond and fullerenes are allotropes of carbon
  • Different arrangements of same element give different properties
Key Understanding: Structure determines properties. Delocalised electrons in graphite and metals allow electrical conduction.

Nanoparticles

  • Size: 1-100 nanometers (1 nm = 1 × 10⁻⁹ m)
  • High surface area to volume ratio = more reactive
  • Applications: Drug delivery, catalysts, sunscreens, electronics

Intermolecular Forces

  • Weak forces between molecules (not within molecules)
  • Stronger in larger molecules (more electrons)
  • Affect boiling/melting points, solubility
  • Much weaker than covalent, ionic, or metallic bonds

Displacement Reactions

  • More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal from compound
  • Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
  • No reaction if less reactive metal added to more reactive metal compound
  • Thermite reaction: Al + Fe₂O₃ → Al₂O₃ + Fe
Worked Example: Will magnesium displace copper from copper sulfate?
Mg is above Cu in reactivity series → YES, reaction occurs

Extraction of Metals

Reduction with Carbon
• Metals below carbon in series
• Heat metal oxide with carbon
• Example: 2Fe₂O₃ + 3C → 4Fe + 3CO₂
• Cheaper method
Thermal Decomposition
• Metals below carbon in series
• Heat breaks down metal compounds
• Used for less reactive metals
• Lower energy requirements

Oxidation and Reduction (Redox)

  • Oxidation: Loss of electrons (OIL) or gain of oxygen
  • Reduction: Gain of electrons (RIG) or loss of oxygen
  • Redox reaction: Both oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously
Worked Example: Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu
Zn → Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻ (oxidation - Zn loses electrons)
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu (reduction - Cu²⁺ gains electrons)

Metal Corrosion

  • Rusting: Iron + oxygen + water → hydrated iron(III) oxide
  • Prevention: Barriers (paint, oil), galvanising (zinc coating), sacrificial protection
  • Galvanising: Zinc more reactive than iron, gets oxidised preferentially

Why are alloys stronger than a pure metal?

  • Alloys: Different sized atoms disrupt the regular structure
  • Result: Layers cannot slide over each other as easily, making alloys harder

Advanced Calculations & Higher Tier Skills

Rate Calculations

Rate = Δ(amount) ÷ Δ(time)
Gradient = Δy ÷ Δx
Worked Example:
At 2 min: 16 cm³ gas produced
At 5 min: 34 cm³ gas produced
Mean rate = (34-16)÷(5-2) = 6 cm³/min

Bond Energy Calculations

ΔH = Σ(bonds broken) - Σ(bonds formed)
Method:
1. Draw out structural formulae
2. Count bonds broken (reactants)
3. Count bonds formed (products)
4. Calculate energy change
Common Error: Forgetting to multiply by number of bonds (e.g., 2 × O-H bonds in H₂O)

Equilibrium Predictions

Step-by-step method:
1. Identify the change
2. Determine which direction opposes it
3. State effect on equilibrium position
4. Explain effect on yield/rate
Industrial Example:
Contact Process: 2SO₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃
ΔH = -196 kJ/mol
• High pressure: favours SO₃ (3→2 moles)
• Moderate temp: compromise yield vs rate
• V₂O₅ catalyst: increases rate only

Extraction Methods

Metal oxide + C → Metal + CO/CO₂
  • Above carbon: Too reactive for carbon reduction
  • Below carbon: Reduction with carbon
  • Cost factors: Energy, raw materials, environmental

Structure-Property Links

  • Conductivity: Freely moving electrons / ions to carry charge
  • Melting / Boiling point: Mention the structure followed by strong electrostatic forces between OR weak intermolecular forces between molecules. ALWAYS end with therefore a small / large amount of energy is needed to overcome
